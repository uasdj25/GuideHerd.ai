# Getting Started with GuideHerd

New to GuideHerd? Start here. Fifteen minutes, and you'll understand what the
system does and who does what.

---

## What GuideHerd does

When someone calls your firm wanting an appointment, two things have to happen:
someone works out what they need, and someone books a time.

GuideHerd takes the second part.

Your receptionist answers the call, works out who's calling and what it's about,
and hands them to GuideHerd's scheduling assistant. The assistant books the
appointment and sends the confirmation. Your receptionist is already free for
the next call.

The caller never repeats themselves — the assistant already knows who they are
before it says hello.

---

## What it doesn't do

Worth being clear up front, because knowing the edges makes the system easier to
trust:

- **It doesn't answer your phones.** A person answers, qualifies the caller, and
  transfers. GuideHerd starts at the transfer.
- **It doesn't give legal advice.** It schedules appointments. Nothing else.
- **It doesn't replace judgment.** When a call needs a person, it says so and
  hands back.
- **It doesn't manage your matters or clients.** It books consultations.

## The pieces

GuideHerd has three screens. Most people use exactly one.

### Reception Console

Where your receptionist works. One form, one button. They enter the caller's
details, prepare the assistant, and transfer.

This is the screen that matters most — it's used on every call.
→ **[Receptionist Guide](receptionist-guide.md)**

### Operations Center

Where you see what's been happening: sessions, bookings, notifications, and
whether anything is going wrong. Read-only — a window, not a control panel.
→ **[Operations Guide](operations-guide.md)**

### Administration

Where your firm's setup lives — practice areas, attorneys, appointment types,
and scheduling rules.
→ **[Administrator Guide](administrator-guide.md)**

---

## Who does what

| Role | Uses | Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Receptionist** | Reception Console | Answers calls, prepares sessions, transfers callers |
| **Administrator** | Administration + Operations | Configures the firm's setup, manages people, handles problems |
| **Operator** | Operations Center | Watches what's happening without changing anything |

One person can hold more than one role. In a small firm, the administrator and
the operator are usually the same person.

## Your first week

### Before you take a single call

1. **Check your firm's setup is right.** Practice areas, attorneys, and
   consultation types must match how your firm actually works. If they don't,
   the receptionist will be choosing from options that don't fit — and that
   error lands on the caller. → [Configuration Guide](configuration-guide.md)

2. **Confirm scheduling rules.** Business hours, timezone, and appointment
   lengths. A wrong timezone is the single most damaging setting to get wrong,
   because everything looks fine until a caller shows up at the wrong hour.

3. **Send a test through end to end.** Prepare a session with your own email,
   transfer, let it book, and check the confirmation arrives. Do this before
   a real caller does it for you.

### First few days

4. **Walk your receptionist through the console.** It takes ten minutes.
   The [Receptionist Guide](receptionist-guide.md) is written to be read in one
   sitting.

5. **Watch the Operations Center daily.** You're looking for failed bookings and
   undelivered notifications. Early patterns are usually setup problems, and
   they're much cheaper to fix in week one than month three.

6. **Ask your receptionist what surprised them.** They'll find the rough edges
   before you do.

---

## The one thing to get right first

**Email addresses.**

The whole flow depends on capturing the caller's email correctly. A typo means a
booked appointment the caller never hears about — and nobody finds out until
they don't show up.

Make reading the address back to the caller a habit from day one. It costs five
seconds and prevents the most common failure in the system.
